Effects of A Norm for Forgiveness on Cooperation and Intergroup Bias

Can relations between individuals from different social groups be improved by changing group norms? Specifically, will a norm that encourages forgiveness increase cooperation with both in- and outgroup members, and will attitude changes predict variation in cooperation? Although psychologists have separately explored relationships between intergroup dynamics and antisocial normative influence, and intergroup dynamics and forgiveness, little empirical work has examined the influence of a prosocial norm, forgiveness, on intergroup cooperation. This project investigates the relationship between an individualâ€™s perception that forgiveness is the common response within their group to transgressions and their cooperative decision-making. Because forgiveness encourages cooperation, because cooperation decreases intergroup bias, and because humans are fundamentally motivated to follow norms, examining this triad in the context of intergroup relations contributes to a growing body of literature on group dynamics and behavior science, and has wider applicability in intervention and public policy development.

Alternative Uses of Forgiveness


Can forgiveness be used in relationships without a history of cooperation, and if so do different cultural emphases on shame and guilt, proximate emotions associated with forgiveness and deterrence, affect how it is employed? Do different cultural emphases on these emotions affect the development of forgiveness in children? Specifically, this study aims to identify the extent to which children from a society that emphasizes shame (Japan) and children from a society that emphasizes guilt (United States) understand that forgiveness can be used to engender these self-conscious emotions in transgressors in order to deter them from imposing future costs and encourage them to confer benefits on the forgiver when the relationship does not have a history of cooperation. While forgiveness may protect cooperative relationships from the negative effects of revenge, this study proposes to explore a possible variant on forgiveness. Instead of being used to maintain cooperative relationships, forgiveness may also be used to establish them.

  • Shame and Guilt, Tangney and Dearing, (2002)
  • Interpersonal consequences of forgiveness: Does forgiveness deter or encourage repeat offenses?, Wallace, Exline and Baumeister, (2008)

A social neuroscience perspective on radicalization

Why do some people become radicalized and regard political violence as a legitimate means of effecting political change? We will examine the underlying social, psychological, and neural mechanisms involved in radicalization and how they facilitate support for political violence. This project will integrate empirical evidence across multiple levels of analysis and methodologies, including social, behavioral, and cognitive neuroscience.

In Year 1, a battery of online questionnaires will measure moral values, political and social attitudes, personality and cognitive styles, and demographic variables from 1,000 individuals in the Chicago area.

In Years 2 and 3, a subset of 160 participants with extreme political views will undergo a MRI scanning session to assess attitudes towards political violence, sensitivity to fairness, and cognitive flexibility. Analyses will integrate dispositional, behavioral, and functional neuroimaging data to test the hypothesis that the strength of interindividual political views is mediated by a limited number of psychological and neurobiological dimensions that are associated with vulnerability to extremism and support for political violence.
